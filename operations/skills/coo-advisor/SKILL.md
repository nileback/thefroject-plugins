# COO Advisor

The COO is the CEO's operational counterpart — making sure the company can deliver what it has promised. The role varies wildly by company; this skill addresses the common patterns.

## Gather context

Ask if not provided:

1. **Stage** — pre-revenue, early revenue, growth, scale, mature?
2. **COO model** — chief of staff, CEO #2 (broad ops), specialist (people / finance / customer)?
3. **Org size** — under 50, 50-200, 200+?
4. **Decision** — process maturity, org structure, scaling, geographic expansion, business reviews?
5. **Pain** — what's broken? (Cross-functional drift, slow execution, cost creep)

## COO archetypes

Three common types:

### 1. CEO #2 — broad ops
Owns everything except product/eng and sales (typically). HR, finance, legal, IT, real estate, internal ops.

### 2. Customer-facing COO
Owns customer experience: support, CS, services, ops. Common in services-heavy or marketplace businesses.

### 3. Strategic COO / COS+
Closer to chief-of-staff role; runs strategic projects, special situations, exec processes.

The advisor approach varies by which type the user is.

## Operational excellence framework

The COO's domain is making the business execute. Five areas matter:

### 1. Cadence
- Daily / weekly / monthly / quarterly / annual rhythms (see `chief-of-staff` for the basic cadence).
- The COO ensures the rhythm exists and people show up.

### 2. Metrics
- Every function has 3-5 KPIs.
- Dashboards exist and are reviewed.
- Trends, not snapshots, drive conversations.

### 3. Process
- Major workflows are documented (sales handoff, customer onboarding, hiring, etc.).
- "Document → optimize → automate" sequence: don't automate broken processes.
- Single source of truth per workflow.

### 4. Org clarity
- Every role has a job description.
- Reporting lines clear; no dotted lines for politics.
- Decision rights documented (RACI for major decisions).

### 5. Communication
- Cross-functional meetings have agendas, owners, follow-ups.
- All-hands cadence consistent.
- Bad news travels up faster than good news.

## Common operational failures

- **Calendar chaos**: leadership team has no shared cadence.
- **Metrics theater**: dashboards exist but nobody uses them.
- **Process drift**: documented processes don't match reality.
- **Org politics**: dotted lines and unclear ownership; everyone touches the work, no one owns it.
- **Communication gaps**: information flows poorly between functions.

The COO's job is identifying and fixing these.

## Scaling considerations

As the company grows past 50, then 200, then 500, operational complexity scales superlinearly.

### 50 people
- Founder-led culture and decisions still possible.
- One-document strategy; everyone reads it.

### 50-200
- Specialized functions emerging (finance, HR, legal).
- Cadence becomes critical; ad-hoc breaks.
- Manager layer thickens; manager quality matters.

### 200-500
- Multi-office or remote-distributed.
- Functional silos start emerging; cross-functional ops becomes critical.
- Process maturity becomes a real investment.
- Culture transmission breaks if not actively managed.

### 500+
- Geographic distribution.
- Acquisition integration.
- Compliance and regulatory complexity.
- Internal services (IT, legal, security) become full functions.

## Cross-functional alignment

When operational drift surfaces, the COO partners with the CEO to realign:

- **Strategic alignment**: see `strategic-alignment` for the OKR-cascade pattern.
- **Operating cadence**: see `chief-of-staff` for the rhythm.
- **Decision rights**: a clear matrix of who decides what.

The COO often runs the leadership team meeting day-to-day; the CEO drops in for strategic decisions.

## Common mistakes

- Confusing the role with chief-of-staff (different scope).
- Building process for process's sake; complexity grows without value.
- Letting metrics decay (visible but ignored).
- Avoiding hard conversations about org clarity / dotted lines.
- Underestimating culture transmission cost as company scales.
