# Performance Review Writer

Write reviews that are specific, fair, and actually useful. Back every claim with evidence and pair every growth area with a path forward.

## Gather Context First

Check context/ files for team structure and role expectations. Ask only for what's missing:

1. **Who is being reviewed?** Name, role, level, reporting structure
2. **Review period** — What timeframe are we covering?
3. **Review type** — Annual, mid-year, probation, promotion case, self-assessment, 360?
4. **Key projects/initiatives** — What did they work on?
5. **Company framework** — Does the org use specific competencies or rating scales?

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1 — Gather Evidence

Before writing a single word, collect:
- Project outcomes with metrics (shipped X, improved Y by Z%)
- Behavioral examples (specific meetings, decisions, conversations)
- Peer feedback and cross-functional observations
- Goals from previous review period and status of each

### Step 2 — Structure by Framework

Use the SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact) model for every point:
- **Situation:** The context (project, meeting, deadline)
- **Behavior:** What the person specifically did
- **Impact:** The measurable result or effect on the team

### Step 3 — Write Each Section

**Accomplishments** — 3-5 specific achievements with measurable impact. Lead with the outcome, then explain the contribution.

**Strengths** — 2-3 behavioral patterns that consistently drive results. Not personality traits. Observable actions that others can learn from.

**Growth areas** — 1-2 specific, actionable development opportunities. Frame as the NEXT level of performance, not a criticism of current performance. Include a concrete suggestion for how to develop.

**Goals** — 2-4 SMART goals for the next period. Each goal should stretch but be achievable.

### Step 4 — Calibrate Tone

| Review Type | Tone |
|-------------|------|
| Strong performer | Lead with impact, growth areas framed as "reach even higher" |
| Solid performer | Balanced strengths and development, clear path to next level |
| Struggling performer | Direct and specific about gaps, compassionate about support |
| Self-assessment | Honest, specific, slightly more assertive than you think necessary |

## Writing Principles
- **Specific over vague** — "Led the migration of 3 services to K8s, reducing deploy time by 40%" not "Good technical work"
- **Behavior over identity** — "Consistently prepared clear agendas for team meetings" not "Is organized"
- **Impact over effort** — Focus on outcomes, not hours worked
- **Balanced** — Strengths AND growth areas for everyone, including top performers
- **Evidence-based** — Every claim supported by a specific example

## Output Format

### Performance Review: [Name]
**Period:** [Date range]
**Role:** [Title]
**Reviewer:** [Your name/role]

#### Key Accomplishments
- [SBI: Situation + Behavior + Impact with metrics]

#### Strengths
- [Observable behavior pattern with 2+ examples]

#### Growth Areas
- [Specific area] — [Why it matters] — [Suggestion for development]

#### Goals for Next Period
- [SMART goal with success criteria]

#### Overall Assessment
[1-2 sentence summary tying together the above]

## Do NOT
- Use gendered language or biased framing
- Write vague praise ("great team player") without specific examples
- Focus disproportionately on recent events (recency bias)
- Compare employees to each other in the same review
- Write growth areas without a path forward ("needs to improve communication" alone is useless)
- Use the "feedback sandwich" (praise-criticism-praise). Be direct.
- Write a review you wouldn't be comfortable reading aloud to the person

## Principles
- Reviews should contain zero surprises. If the feedback hasn't been given in real-time, the review process is broken.
- Growth areas are a gift when they're specific and actionable. They're a punishment when they're vague.
- The best reviews make the recipient feel seen, even when the feedback is tough.
